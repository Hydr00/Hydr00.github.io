Origin

The word "hacker" is a transliteration of English Hacker. The word existed as early as Shakespeare, but the first time people really understood it was after the advent of computers. According to the Oxford English Dictionary, the earliest meaning of the word "hack" was chopping, which is easily reminiscent of computers being hacked by others. So the Oxford English Dictionary explains that the word "Hacker" involves the meaning of a computer: "people who use their computer skills to try to access computer files or networks without authorization."

The earliest computers were born at the University of Pennsylvania in 1946, and the earliest hackers appeared at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. So does Bell Labs. The original hackers were generally senior technicians who were keen to challenge, advocate freedom and advocate the sharing of information.

Since 1994, the rapid development of the Internet in China and even the world has provided people with convenience, freedom and unlimited wealth. Politics, military, economy, science and technology, education, culture and other aspects are becoming more and more networked, and gradually become a part of people's life and entertainment. It can be said that the information age has arrived, information has become the third resource to maintain human society in addition to material and energy, it is an important medium in the future life. With the popularity of computers and the rapid development of Internet technology, hackers also appeared.

Historical significance

For a long time, there has been a shared cultural community of expert programmers and web masters, dating back to the first time-sharing minicomputer and the earliest ARPAnet experiments in the 1960s. Participants in the culture coined the word "hacker". Hackers built Internet. Hackers made the Unix operating system what it is today. The hackers set up Usenet. The hackers made it work. If you are part of this culture, if you have contributed to it, and if other people in the circle know who you are and call you a hacker, then you are a hacker.

The spirit of hacking is not limited to the cultural circle of software hackers. Some people also treat other things like electronics and music as hackers-in fact, you can find it in any higher level of science and art. Software hackers identify their peers in other areas and call them hackers-some claim that hackers are actually independent of their work. But in this article, we focus on the technology and attitude of software hackers and the shared cultural traditions that invented the word "hacker."
